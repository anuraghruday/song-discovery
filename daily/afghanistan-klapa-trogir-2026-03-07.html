Klapa Trogir

It is 2019, and the empires are packing their bags, though they do not quite know it yet. At Camp Lion in Kabul, the air tastes of pulverized granite, diesel exhaust, and the metallic tang of impending defeat.

A Croatian soldier, part of the NATO Resolute Support mission, sits on a Hesco barrier looking toward the jagged, snow-capped spine of the Hindu Kush. To drown out the hum of American Black Hawks and the distant, rhythmic thud of artillery, he pulls out his phone and plays a song from home.

It is an a cappella track. A group of men, standing shoulder to shoulder in a stone courtyard thousands of miles away, inhale in unison. And then, they sing.

Part I The Anatomy of Exhaustion

"Pute, laze pizon dubli tovari..."

(Paths and field entrances worn deep by burdened donkeys.)

In the high altitudes of the Hindu Kush, the donkey remains the undisputed king of logistics. Pick your way up the Salang Pass, or navigate the treacherous, narrow defiles of the Wakhan Corridor, and you will see them. Their hooves wear deep grooves into the ancient rock, just as they did when Alexander the Great marched his armies through these exact same valleys in 330 BC.

Afghanistan is a country defined by its thirst. The rivers that rush down from the Pamir knot are cold and violent, but the plains are parched. For thousands of years, the Afghan people have survived using karez—subterranean aqueducts dug by hand deep into the arid earth. They are the lifelines of the desert, fiercely protected and deeply revered.

"Gustirne žedne mijun sići i lati..."

The Graveyard & The Karst

The juxtaposition of Dalmatia and Afghanistan might seem absurd on a map. One is a Mediterranean paradise; the other, a landlocked, war-torn expanse of Central Asia.

But in their bones, they share the exact same historical exhaustion. Both regions are defined by unforgiving topographies that empires covet but cannot tame.

The Balkans of Asia

Dalmatia has the Dinaric Alps, a wall of limestone. Afghanistan has the Hindu Kush, a tectonic collision zone that spikes 25,000 feet into the troposphere.

Afghanistan is not just a country; it is a verb. It is something that happens to empires. And yet, the people who actually live there are the ones left to sweep up the ashes. Crushed by the weight of their own history.

The Smog and the Stone

If the Hindu Kush is the fortress, Kabul is its chaotic, beating heart. A city that has been destroyed and rebuilt so many times it has lost count.

It smells of burning coal, cheap diesel, incinerated plastic, and the rich, savory smoke of lamb fat sizzling over street-side braziers.
It smells of survival.

"Izlizana Bašćanska tavuleta / 'rvackeg ditinjevanja..."
(The faded Baška tablet / relics of Croatian childhood)

How many times has Afghanistan had its identity overwritten? The Greco-Bactrian Buddhas blown to dust. The British Durand Line. The Soviet brutalist blocks. The American biometric databases. Yet, the Afghan clings to the poetry that has sustained them through the dark ages.

July 2019

A suicide bomber slammed into a Croatian military convoy on the outskirts of Kabul. A 27-year-old soldier named Josip Briški was killed. The dust of the Balkans and the dust of Central Asia mixed in the most tragic, literal way.

The empires always leave. They look at their watches. The Afghans look at the mountains. They know that time is measured not in deployments, but in geology.
